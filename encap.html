Encapsulation

Encapsulation in Object-Oriented Programming (OOP) is the concept of bundling the data (variables) and the methods (functions) that operate on that data into a single unit, usually a class. This principle is crucial for protecting the integrity of the data and ensuring that it can only be modified in controlled ways.
To illustrate encapsulation with a real-life example, imagine a company that has different departments, like accounts, finance, and sales. Each department manages its own data and operations. The finance department handles all the financial transactions and maintains records of them, while the sales department manages and records all sales activities. If someone in the finance department needs sales data, they cannot directly access the sales department's data. Instead, they must go through the proper channelsâ€”by contacting a person in the sales department who can then provide the necessary data.
This is a perfect analogy for encapsulation in programming. In this scenario, the sales data and the operations that can manipulate it are encapsulated within the "sales section." The data is protected, and only specific methods (in this case, through the appropriate personnel) can access or modify it.
In C++, encapsulation is achieved by using classes. The data members (variables) of a class are typically marked as `private` or `protected`, which means they cannot be accessed directly from outside the class. The only way to interact with the data is through public methods (functions) defined in the class. This design ensures that the internal state of an object can only be changed in predefined ways, helping to prevent unintended interference and errors.
Moreover, encapsulation naturally leads to data abstraction or data hiding. In the example, the details of how sales data is stored and managed are hidden from the finance department, just as the internal implementation details of a class are hidden from the outside world in programming. This separation of concerns makes systems more modular, easier to understand, and safer to work with.
